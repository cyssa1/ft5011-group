DATASET DESCRIPTION: technical_dataset.csv
==========================================


OVERVIEW
--------
The dataset contains daily observations for 10 large-capitalisation US technology stocks —
Apple (AAPL), Microsoft (MSFT), NVIDIA (NVDA), Oracle (ORCL), Cisco (CSCO), IBM (IBM),
AMD (AMD), Salesforce (CRM), Intel (INTC), and Qualcomm (QCOM) — covering the period from
10 February 2015 to 23 December 2024. After removing the warm-up period required by the
slowest technical indicator (approximately 35 trading days) and the trailing five rows per
ticker whose forward labels would require unseen future data, the dataset contains 24,850
rows and 23 columns. Each row represents one trading day for one ticker.

The data was sourced from Yahoo Finance using the yfinance Python library and uses
split- and dividend-adjusted prices throughout all indicator calculations to ensure that
historical signals are not distorted by corporate actions such as stock splits or dividend
distributions.


COLUMN REFERENCE
----------------

1. Date (Index)
   The trading date in YYYY-MM-DD format. This is the row index of the dataset. Only
   trading days are present; weekends and US market holidays are excluded. The date
   represents the day on which the observation was recorded, and all features on that row
   are constructed exclusively from data available on or before that date. No future
   information leaks into any feature column.

2. Ticker
   The stock's exchange ticker symbol (e.g., AAPL, NVDA). All 10 tickers are present in
   the same file. This column is essential for grouping observations by company when
   training per-ticker models or when computing sequence windows for an LSTM — each
   ticker's time series must be treated independently to avoid mixing price histories
   across different companies.


PRICE COLUMNS (RAW AND ADJUSTED)
---------------------------------
All price columns with the Adj_ prefix are adjusted for stock splits and dividend payments.
These are the prices used in all technical indicator calculations. The unadjusted columns
(Open, High, Low, Close) are retained for reference and for computing position sizes or
entry/exit prices in a simulated trading context.

3. Open
   The unadjusted opening price of the stock on that trading day, in US dollars.

4. High
   The unadjusted intraday high price reached during that trading day.

5. Low
   The unadjusted intraday low price reached during that trading day.

6. Close
   The unadjusted closing price of the stock at market close.

7. Adj_Open
   The split- and dividend-adjusted opening price. Computed by multiplying Open by the
   adjustment factor Adj_Close / Close. Using adjusted prices ensures that a 2-for-1 stock
   split does not appear as a 50% price drop in the historical series and does not corrupt
   momentum indicators computed across that date.

8. Adj_High
   The adjusted intraday high, computed analogously to Adj_Open.

9. Adj_Low
   The adjusted intraday low, computed analogously to Adj_Open.

10. Adj_Close
    The fully adjusted closing price. This is the primary price series used in all 10
    technical indicator computations. It is the standard reference price in academic and
    practitioner work on technical analysis because it produces a continuous, comparable
    time series across corporate events.

11. Volume
    Raw daily trading volume (number of shares traded). Volume is not adjusted by the
    price adjustment factor — volume ratios are self-normalising (each value is divided by
    its own rolling mean), so the absolute scale is irrelevant and no adjustment is needed.


TECHNICAL INDICATOR COLUMNS
-----------------------------
All 10 indicators are calculated independently per ticker before the tickers are
concatenated into the final dataset. This ensures no cross-contamination occurs between
companies. All indicators use only data available up to and including the current row
(no look-ahead). Rows where any indicator is still in its warm-up window and would produce
a NaN (approximately the first 35 trading days per ticker) are dropped.

12. rsi_14 — Relative Strength Index (14-day)

    What it is:
    A bounded momentum oscillator ranging from 0 to 100. Values above 70 are
    conventionally interpreted as overbought; values below 30 as oversold.

    Why it is included:
    RSI is one of the most empirically validated short-horizon momentum indicators.
    Because it is bounded and does not require further scaling, it is particularly
    well-suited as a machine learning feature — it cannot produce extreme outliers
    regardless of the stock's absolute price level. It provides the model with a compact
    signal about whether a stock has recently risen too sharply (mean reversion candidate)
    or declined too sharply (bounce candidate).

    Calculation:
    For each day, the price change is decomposed into gains (positive changes) and losses
    (absolute negative changes). A 14-period exponential moving average with smoothing
    factor alpha = 1/14 (Wilder's smoothing) is applied separately to the gain series and
    the loss series, producing average gain (AG) and average loss (AL).
    RS = AG / AL
    RSI = 100 - (100 / (1 + RS))

13. macd_hist — MACD Histogram (12, 26, 9)

    What it is:
    The difference between the MACD line and the MACD signal line. The MACD line is the
    difference between a 12-period and a 26-period EMA of the closing price. The signal
    line is a 9-period EMA of the MACD line. The histogram is therefore the second
    derivative of the price trend — it captures the rate of change of momentum rather than
    momentum itself.

    Why it is included:
    The histogram crosses zero before the MACD line crosses its signal line, making it a
    leading indicator of momentum shifts. For a 5-day prediction horizon, catching momentum
    reversals early is directly valuable. Using the histogram rather than the raw MACD line
    avoids scale dependence (the raw MACD value is proportional to the stock's absolute
    price level, which varies enormously across tickers).

    Calculation:
    EMA_fast   = EMA(Adj_Close, span=12)
    EMA_slow   = EMA(Adj_Close, span=26)
    MACD_line  = EMA_fast - EMA_slow
    Signal_line = EMA(MACD_line, span=9)
    MACD_Histogram = MACD_line - Signal_line
    All EMAs use adjust=False (recursive initialisation).

14. roc_5 — Rate of Change (5-day)

    What it is:
    The percentage price change over the past 5 trading days.
    ROC_5 = (Adj_Close_t / Adj_Close_{t-5}) - 1

    Why it is included:
    The forward prediction target is defined over a 5-day horizon. The 5-day backward
    return is therefore the natural momentum feature: it tests whether the stock exhibits
    momentum persistence (recent winners continue to win) or mean reversion (recent winners
    give back gains). Including this feature allows the model to distinguish between these
    two regimes. It is deliberately calibrated to match the prediction horizon rather than
    using an arbitrary window. Values are expressed as decimals (0.03 = +3%).

    Calculation:
    ROC_5 = (Adj_Close_t / Adj_Close_{t-5}) - 1

15. stoch_k — Stochastic Oscillator %K (14, 3)

    What it is:
    A bounded momentum oscillator ranging from 0 to 100 that locates the current closing
    price relative to the recent high-low range. A value of 100 means the close is at the
    highest high of the past 14 days; a value of 0 means it is at the lowest low.

    Why it is included:
    Unlike RSI, which is computed solely from closing prices, the Stochastic oscillator
    incorporates the full intraday range (high and low). This gives the model information
    about intraday volatility structure that RSI cannot provide. The two indicators are
    complementary — RSI captures close-to-close momentum exhaustion while Stochastic
    captures whether the close is near the boundary of recent price action. Including both
    provides two orthogonal overbought/oversold perspectives.

    Calculation:
    Lowest_Low   = rolling minimum of Adj_Low over 14 days
    Highest_High = rolling maximum of Adj_High over 14 days
    Raw_%K       = 100 * (Adj_Close - Lowest_Low) / (Highest_High - Lowest_Low)
    Stoch_%K     = 3-day simple moving average of Raw_%K  (slow stochastic, reduces noise)

16. adx_14 — Average Directional Index (14-day)

    What it is:
    A non-directional trend strength indicator ranging from 0 to 100. Values above 25
    conventionally indicate a trending market; values below 20 indicate a ranging or
    sideways market. Crucially, ADX does not encode direction — it only measures the
    strength of the prevailing trend.

    Why it is included:
    ADX is the only feature in the set that tells the model about the quality of the
    market regime rather than the direction of recent price movement. Momentum signals like
    RSI and MACD are reliable in trending markets and generate false signals in sideways
    markets. By including ADX, the model can learn to weight momentum features more heavily
    when ADX is high and discount them when ADX is low, effectively performing implicit
    regime detection. This is a well-documented interaction effect in the technical analysis
    literature.

    Calculation:
    TR      = max(Adj_High - Adj_Low, |Adj_High - PrevAdj_Close|, |Adj_Low - PrevAdj_Close|)
    +DM     = max(Adj_High - PrevAdj_High, 0) when it exceeds -DM, else 0
    -DM     = max(PrevAdj_Low - Adj_Low,  0) when it exceeds +DM, else 0
    ATR     = 14-period Wilder EMA of TR
    +DI     = 100 * EMA(+DM, 14) / ATR
    -DI     = 100 * EMA(-DM, 14) / ATR
    DX      = 100 * |+DI - -DI| / (+DI + -DI)
    ADX     = 14-period Wilder EMA of DX

17. atr_norm_14 — Normalised Average True Range (14-day)

    What it is:
    The Average True Range divided by the closing price, expressed as a proportion. This
    produces a percentage measure of daily volatility that is comparable across tickers
    with very different absolute price levels (e.g., NVDA at $800 vs CSCO at $55).

    Why it is included:
    Volatility is a first-order context variable for any directional prediction. A 1% daily
    move in a low-volatility stock is a strong signal; the same move in a high-volatility
    stock is noise. By including normalised ATR, the model gains access to the current
    volatility regime for each ticker. Low-volatility consolidation periods (low ATR) are
    historically followed by directional breakouts, which are directly relevant to 5-day
    forward prediction. Normalising by the closing price makes this feature scale-free and
    cross-ticker comparable.

    Calculation:
    TR       = max(Adj_High - Adj_Low, |Adj_High - PrevAdj_Close|, |Adj_Low - PrevAdj_Close|)
    ATR      = 14-period Wilder EMA of TR
    ATR_Norm = ATR / Adj_Close

18. bb_pct_20 — Bollinger Band %B (20-day, 2 standard deviations)

    What it is:
    The position of the closing price within its 20-day Bollinger Bands, expressed as a
    value between 0 (at the lower band) and 1 (at the upper band). Values above 1 indicate
    the price is above the upper band; values below 0 indicate it is below the lower band.

    Why it is included:
    Unlike RSI which is purely a function of price changes, Bollinger %B is
    volatility-adjusted — the bands expand during high-volatility periods and contract
    during low-volatility periods. A close near the upper band means the price is extended
    relative to its own recent volatility distribution, not just relative to a fixed
    threshold. This makes it a more adaptive overbought/oversold indicator than RSI.
    Including both RSI and %B provides the model with two theoretically complementary
    signals about mean reversion pressure.

    Calculation:
    Middle_Band = 20-day simple moving average of Adj_Close
    Std         = 20-day population standard deviation of Adj_Close (ddof=0)
    Upper_Band  = Middle_Band + 2 * Std
    Lower_Band  = Middle_Band - 2 * Std
    %B          = (Adj_Close - Lower_Band) / (Upper_Band - Lower_Band)

19. ema_ratio_20 — EMA Ratio (Close / EMA-20)

    What it is:
    The ratio of the current adjusted closing price to its 20-day exponential moving
    average. A value of 1.0 means the price is exactly at its 20-day EMA; values above
    1.0 indicate the price is above its short-term trend; values below 1.0 indicate it
    is below.

    Why it is included:
    This is one of the simplest and most robust trend features in the ML-on-price
    literature. By expressing price as a ratio to its trend rather than in absolute terms,
    the feature is naturally normalised and dimensionless — it is directly comparable
    across all 10 tickers without any further scaling. Empirically, price-to-moving-average
    ratios consistently rank among the top features in decision tree and random forest
    models trained on price data.

    Calculation:
    EMA_20    = 20-day exponential moving average of Adj_Close (adjust=False)
    EMA_Ratio = Adj_Close / EMA_20

20. obv_roc_5 — On-Balance Volume Rate of Change (5-day)

    What it is:
    The 5-day rate of change of the On-Balance Volume (OBV) indicator. OBV itself is a
    running cumulative sum that adds the day's volume when the close is up and subtracts
    it when the close is down, creating a volume-weighted directional indicator.

    Why it is included:
    Volume is the only feature in this set that is entirely independent of price direction
    — it measures participation and conviction behind a price move. The OBV Rate of Change
    captures whether volume momentum is accelerating in the same direction as price
    (confirming the trend) or diverging from it (potential reversal signal). Price moves
    accompanied by strong volume momentum are more likely to persist; price moves on
    declining OBV are more likely to reverse. The 5-day window is deliberately aligned to
    the prediction horizon. Using ROC rather than raw OBV removes the scale and cumulative
    drift issues of the raw OBV series.

    Calculation:
    Direction  = sign(Adj_Close_t - Adj_Close_{t-1})   (+1, -1, or 0)
    OBV        = cumulative sum of (Direction * Volume)
    OBV_ROC_5  = (OBV_t / OBV_{t-5}) - 1

21. vol_ratio_20 — Volume Ratio (Volume / 20-day Average Volume)

    What it is:
    Today's trading volume divided by the 20-day simple moving average of volume. A value
    of 1.0 represents average activity; values above 2.0 indicate an unusual volume spike;
    values below 0.5 indicate unusually thin trading.

    Why it is included:
    While OBV ROC captures cumulative volume momentum, the Volume Ratio captures single-day
    anomalies. Abnormally high volume on a given day often coincides with institutional
    activity, news events, or exhaustion at price extremes — all of which are directly
    relevant to near-term price direction. This feature provides a complementary,
    non-cumulative perspective on volume compared to OBV ROC. Because it is expressed as
    a ratio, it is scale-free and directly comparable across tickers regardless of their
    float or average daily volume.

    Calculation:
    Vol_MA_20  = 20-day simple moving average of Volume
    Vol_Ratio  = Volume / Vol_MA_20


LABEL COLUMNS
-------------

22. signal
    A categorical string label: BUY, HOLD, or SELL. Generated by the forward_5day
    labelling strategy described below. Used for human-readable reporting and distribution
    analysis.

23. label
    An integer encoding of signal: BUY = 1, HOLD = 0, SELL = 2. This is the target
    variable passed to all model training pipelines.

24. fwd_ret_5d
    The raw forward 5-day return: (Adj_Close_{t+5} / Adj_Close_t) - 1. This column is
    NOT used as a feature during training. It is retained exclusively for post-hoc trading
    strategy evaluation — specifically for computing the simulated cumulative return,
    Sharpe ratio, and hit rate of the model's predictions on the held-out test set.


LABELLING METHODOLOGY
---------------------
Labels are generated using the forward_5day strategy. For each trading day t:

  BUY  — Adj_Close is strictly higher than Adj_Close_t on ALL FIVE of the following
         trading days (t+1, t+2, t+3, t+4, t+5).

  SELL — Adj_Close is strictly lower than Adj_Close_t on ALL FIVE of the following
         trading days.

  HOLD — All other cases (mixed direction, flat, or only partially trending).

This definition is intentionally strict. Requiring all five days to be consistently above
or below the current close selects high-confidence directional moves and reduces label
noise compared to a single-day or net-return threshold. It aligns the label definition
with a realistic trading rule: a BUY signal means an investor who enters at today's close
and holds for five days will be in profit on every intermediate day, not just the final day.

No look-ahead bias is introduced. The label for row t uses only prices from t+1 through
t+5. None of the 10 feature columns use any data beyond time t. The last five rows of
each ticker are dropped from the dataset entirely because their labels would require
prices beyond the available data range and would be undefined.

Label distribution across all 10 tickers combined:
  HOLD : 12,805 rows  (51.5%)
  BUY  :  7,026 rows  (28.3%)
  SELL :  5,019 rows  (20.2%)

The moderate class imbalance — particularly the under-representation of SELL relative to
BUY — reflects the long-term upward drift of large-cap tech stocks over the 2015-2024
period. This imbalance is addressed during model training through class-weighted loss
functions, and model performance is evaluated using macro-averaged F1 score rather than
accuracy alone to avoid the majority-class bias that accuracy would introduce.
