DATASET DESCRIPTION: fundamental_dataset.csv
=============================================


OVERVIEW
--------
The dataset contains daily fundamental financial metrics for 10 large-capitalisation US
technology stocks — Apple (AAPL), Microsoft (MSFT), NVIDIA (NVDA), Oracle (ORCL),
Cisco (CSCO), IBM (IBM), AMD (AMD), Salesforce (CRM), Intel (INTC), and Qualcomm (QCOM)
— covering the period from 1 January 2015 to 31 December 2024. The dataset contains
26,090 rows and 18 columns, with 2,609 business days per ticker.

The raw data was sourced from US SEC EDGAR quarterly filings (10-Q and 10-K) via the
file 02_edgar_quarterly.csv. EDGAR provides standardised XBRL-tagged financial
statements submitted directly by companies to the SEC. Each company files four reports
per year (three 10-Q filings and one 10-K), giving approximately 60 quarterly
observations per ticker over the 2010–2024 period. After computing financial ratios
from the raw statement items and forward-filling to a daily business-day frequency, the
result is a daily feature set that reflects the most recently available published
financials for each trading day.


SOURCE DATA AND FILING STRUCTURE
---------------------------------
Source file : data/fundamental data/02_edgar_quarterly.csv
Raw shape   : 1,803 rows (approximately 60 quarters × 10 tickers + 19 additional tickers)
Columns     : 82 raw XBRL-tagged fields from income, balance, and cash flow statements

Each row in the source file represents one quarterly filing for one company. The end_date
field indicates the last day of the fiscal quarter covered by that filing. Because EDGAR
filings are not available to the public on the quarter end date — companies have up to
40 days (10-Q) or 60 days (10-K) to file — a conservative publication lag of 45 days
was applied to all observations before forward-filling to the daily series. This means
that a quarterly report with end_date = 2023-06-30 is considered available in the daily
dataset starting from 2023-08-14, not from 2023-06-30. This prevents any look-ahead
bias from financial data reaching the model before it would have been publicly available.


REVENUE FIELD COALESCING
-------------------------
Different companies use different XBRL tags for their top-line revenue figure in EDGAR
filings. To obtain a single consistent revenue series, the following priority order was
applied: RevenueFromContractWithCustomerExcludingAssessedTax (used by most modern
filings) → Revenues (used by older filings) → SalesRevenueNet (legacy tag). The first
non-null value across these three fields was used for each row.


MISSING VALUE PATTERN (STRUCTURAL)
------------------------------------
Some columns have structurally high NaN rates for specific tickers because those
companies use non-standard XBRL tags that do not map to the fields extracted:

  - IBM: OperatingIncomeLoss is 100% NaN because IBM historically reports operating
    results under non-standard XBRL segment labels rather than the standard tag.
    As a result, op_margin is fully missing for IBM.

  - QCOM: GrossProfit is 100% NaN because Qualcomm does not file a gross profit line
    item separately in their EDGAR submissions. gross_margin and fcf_margin are
    therefore fully missing for QCOM.

  - NVDA: fcf_margin is approximately 56% NaN due to inconsistent reporting of capital
    expenditures (PaymentsToAcquirePropertyPlantAndEquipment) across filing periods.

  - Other tickers show 10-30% NaN rates in individual columns, primarily from quarters
    where specific line items were not separately disclosed.

These NaNs are structural and reflect genuine gaps in EDGAR XBRL data rather than
errors in the dataset. They are handled in the machine learning pipeline via
column-level median imputation using the non-null values across all tickers and
time periods. The remaining ~70-90% of data per column is complete and usable.


FORWARD-FILL METHODOLOGY
--------------------------
Quarterly fundamentals are only updated four times per year. To create a daily feature
series that can be joined to daily price and technical data, each quarterly ratio value
is carried forward to every business day until the next quarterly filing becomes
available. The procedure is:

  1. For each quarterly observation, compute effective_date = end_date + 45 days.
  2. Create a daily scaffold of all business days from 2015-01-01 to 2024-12-31.
  3. For each trading day, use pandas merge_asof (backward direction) to assign the
     most recently available quarterly filing whose effective_date is on or before
     that trading day.
  4. Trading days that fall before any filing's effective_date for a given ticker
     (the very start of the series) receive NaN values.

This approach ensures that the model sees only the most recent publicly available
quarterly figures on any given day, consistent with how a real investor would have
access to fundamental data.

The end_date column in the output records which quarterly filing period the
forward-filled values originate from, providing full traceability.


COLUMN REFERENCE
----------------

1. Ticker
   The stock's exchange ticker symbol. Used as the join key to other datasets.

2. Date
   The trading date (business day) in YYYY-MM-DD format. Covers 2015-01-01 to
   2024-12-31. This is the primary join key when merging with technical_dataset.csv
   and sentiment_dataset.csv.

3. end_date
   The fiscal quarter end date of the most recently available EDGAR filing as of
   this Date row. For example, if Date = 2023-09-15, end_date might be 2023-06-30
   (the Q2 filing, whose effective_date = 2023-08-14 has passed). This column is
   not a model feature — it provides audit trail and transparency.


PROFITABILITY RATIO COLUMNS
-----------------------------

4. gross_margin

   What it is:
   Gross profit divided by total revenue: gross_margin = GrossProfit / Revenue.
   Represents the percentage of revenue retained after direct production costs
   (cost of goods sold). A gross margin of 0.70 means the company retains 70 cents
   of every dollar of revenue before operating expenses.

   Why it is included:
   Gross margin is one of the most stable and persistent fundamental quality signals
   for technology companies. High-margin software and platform businesses (like MSFT
   or CRM) command higher valuations and are more resilient in downturns. Changes in
   gross margin quarter-over-quarter can signal pricing power shifts, competitive
   pressure, or product mix changes — all of which affect near-term stock performance.

   Calculation: GrossProfit / Revenue (coalesced revenue field).
   NaN rate: Approximately 9-26% for most tickers; 78% for ORCL; 100% for QCOM.

5. op_margin

   What it is:
   Operating income divided by revenue: op_margin = OperatingIncomeLoss / Revenue.
   Captures profitability after all operating expenses (cost of revenue, R&D, SG&A)
   but before interest and taxes.

   Why it is included:
   Operating margin reflects a company's core business efficiency independent of its
   capital structure. For technology companies with heavy R&D spending, operating
   margin is a key indicator of whether the company is scaling efficiently. Improvement
   in operating margin across quarters is a strong positive signal; compression
   indicates rising cost pressure or revenue slowdown.

   Calculation: OperatingIncomeLoss / Revenue.
   NaN rate: 10-26% for most tickers; 100% for IBM (structural, see above).

6. net_margin

   What it is:
   Net income divided by revenue: net_margin = NetIncomeLoss / Revenue. The bottom-line
   percentage of revenue that flows through to shareholders after all expenses, interest,
   and taxes.

   Why it is included:
   Net margin is the most comprehensive measure of profitability. While it can be
   distorted by one-time items and tax events, it remains a core valuation input and
   is directly related to earnings per share growth. Net margin expansion typically
   precedes positive analyst revisions and price appreciation.

   Calculation: NetIncomeLoss / Revenue.
   NaN rate: 4-26% depending on ticker.

7. roa — Return on Assets (annualised)

   What it is:
   Annualised net income divided by total assets: roa = (NetIncomeLoss × 4) / Assets.
   Measures how efficiently a company generates profit from its asset base. A value
   of 0.15 means the company earns 15 cents for every dollar of assets annually.

   Why it is included:
   ROA normalises profitability by the size of the asset base, making it directly
   comparable across companies with very different scales (e.g., AAPL's $345B asset
   base vs QCOM's $28B). Rising ROA indicates improving capital efficiency; falling ROA
   may signal over-investment in assets without commensurate profit growth.

   Calculation: (NetIncomeLoss × 4) / Assets. Multiplied by 4 to annualise quarterly
   net income. Clipped to [-5, 5] to remove extreme outliers.

8. roe — Return on Equity (annualised)

   What it is:
   Annualised net income divided by stockholders' equity: roe = (NetIncomeLoss × 4)
   / StockholdersEquity. Measures the return generated for shareholders on their
   invested capital.

   Why it is included:
   ROE is the primary measure of shareholder value creation. High and rising ROE
   (especially when not driven by excessive leverage) is a consistent positive
   predictor of forward returns. For tech companies with large buyback programs, ROE
   can be very high due to reduced equity base — including debt_ratio alongside ROE
   allows the model to distinguish quality ROE from leverage-driven ROE.

   Calculation: (NetIncomeLoss × 4) / StockholdersEquity. Clipped to [-5, 5].
   Note: AMD had negative stockholders' equity before 2018, producing negative ROE
   values that the clip handles.


LEVERAGE COLUMNS
-----------------

9. debt_ratio

   What it is:
   Long-term debt divided by total assets: debt_ratio = LongTermDebt / Assets.
   Measures the proportion of assets financed by long-term borrowings.

   Why it is included:
   The level of financial leverage affects a company's vulnerability to rising interest
   rates, credit downgrades, and earnings volatility. For technology companies that
   issue debt to fund buybacks (common at AAPL, MSFT), a rising debt ratio in
   conjunction with high ROE is not necessarily negative. The model can learn this
   nuanced interaction. Additionally, debt ratio shifts often precede credit events
   that can sharply reprice equities.

   Calculation: LongTermDebtNoncurrent / Assets (falls back to LongTermDebt if
   noncurrent variant is unavailable). Clipped to [0, 5].

10. debt_to_equity

    What it is:
    Long-term debt divided by stockholders' equity: debt_to_equity = LongTermDebt
    / StockholdersEquity. A complementary leverage measure that shows how much debt
    is carried per dollar of equity.

    Why it is included:
    Debt-to-equity captures leverage from the equity perspective. It is more sensitive
    than debt_ratio when a company's equity base is shrinking (e.g., through buybacks),
    which is common in large-cap tech. Including both debt_ratio and debt_to_equity
    allows the model to detect these capital structure dynamics.

    Calculation: LongTermDebt / StockholdersEquity. Clipped to [-5, 5].


EFFICIENCY AND QUALITY COLUMNS
--------------------------------

11. rd_intensity

    What it is:
    Research and development expense divided by revenue: rd_intensity = R&D / Revenue.
    Measures the fraction of revenue reinvested in future product development.

    Why it is included:
    For technology companies, R&D is the primary driver of future competitive advantage.
    Changes in R&D intensity signal strategic shifts: a sudden increase may precede a
    major product cycle (positive); a sharp decrease in a growth company may signal
    cost-cutting under duress (negative). R&D intensity is also a key input in many
    academic asset pricing models for technology stocks.

    Calculation: ResearchAndDevelopmentExpense / Revenue. Clipped to [0, 5].
    NaN rate: 10-25% for most tickers.

12. fcf_margin

    What it is:
    Free cash flow margin: fcf_margin = (OperatingCashFlow - CapEx) / Revenue.
    Measures the percentage of revenue converted to free cash available to shareholders
    after maintaining and growing the asset base.

    Why it is included:
    Free cash flow is widely considered the most reliable measure of a company's true
    profitability because it is harder to manipulate through accounting choices than net
    income. High FCF margin companies have the financial flexibility to return capital
    to shareholders (buybacks, dividends) or make strategic acquisitions, both of which
    are positive for the stock price. FCF margin is particularly important for evaluating
    capital-intensive semiconductor companies (NVDA, INTC, QCOM) vs. asset-light
    software companies (CRM, MSFT).

    Calculation: (NetCashProvidedByUsedInOperatingActivities -
    PaymentsToAcquirePropertyPlantAndEquipment.abs()) / Revenue. CapEx is stored as a
    positive payment figure and subtracted from operating cash flow. Clipped to [-5, 5].
    NaN rate: ~25% for most tickers; 56% for NVDA; 100% for QCOM (structural).

13. asset_turnover

    What it is:
    Annualised revenue divided by total assets: asset_turnover = (Revenue × 4) / Assets.
    Measures how efficiently a company generates revenue from its asset base.

    Why it is included:
    Asset turnover distinguishes asset-intensive businesses (low turnover: semiconductor
    fabs like INTC) from asset-light platforms (high turnover: software-as-a-service
    companies like CRM). Combined with net margin, asset turnover forms the DuPont
    decomposition of ROA, and declining asset turnover in a mature company can be an
    early warning of competitive erosion or overcapitalisation.

    Calculation: (Revenue × 4) / Assets. Multiplied by 4 to annualise quarterly revenue.
    Clipped to [0, 5].


GROWTH COLUMNS
---------------

14. rev_growth_qoq

    What it is:
    Quarter-over-quarter revenue growth: (Revenue_t / Revenue_{t-1}) - 1. Measures
    the sequential percentage change in revenue from the previous quarter.

    Why it is included:
    Sequential growth captures the immediate momentum of a company's business. Positive
    QoQ growth in a typically seasonal quarter, or deceleration in an historically
    strong quarter, are both important signals. For 5-day forward prediction, QoQ
    revenue momentum is a strong short-horizon predictor because it feeds analyst
    estimate revisions which drive near-term price moves.

    Calculation: revenue.pct_change(1) per ticker. Clipped to [-5, 5].
    NaN rate: 0% (growth is computed before forward-filling, so first-quarter NaNs
    from the warm-up period are resolved via the merge_asof daily expansion).

15. rev_growth_yoy

    What it is:
    Year-over-year revenue growth: (Revenue_t / Revenue_{t-4}) - 1. Compares the
    current quarter to the same quarter one year ago, removing seasonal effects.

    Why it is included:
    YoY growth is the standard benchmark reported by companies and tracked by Wall
    Street analysts. It removes seasonal patterns (e.g., AAPL's holiday quarter is
    always stronger than Q2) that QoQ growth cannot, providing a cleaner view of
    underlying business trajectory. Acceleration or deceleration in YoY growth
    are primary drivers of analyst rating changes.

    Calculation: revenue.pct_change(4) per ticker. Clipped to [-5, 5].

16. ni_growth_qoq

    What it is:
    Quarter-over-quarter net income growth: (NetIncome_t / NetIncome_{t-1}) - 1.

    Why it is included:
    Net income growth is more volatile than revenue growth due to operating leverage
    and one-time items, but it directly determines EPS and therefore the denominator
    of the P/E ratio. Rapid QoQ net income growth often leads to P/E multiple
    expansion, which is a positive price catalyst.

    Calculation: net_income.pct_change(1) per ticker. Clipped to [-5, 5].
    NaN rate: ~1.6% (from quarters where net income crosses zero, making
    pct_change undefined).

17. ni_growth_yoy

    What it is:
    Year-over-year net income growth: (NetIncome_t / NetIncome_{t-4}) - 1. The YoY
    analogue of ni_growth_qoq, removing seasonal effects.

    Why it is included:
    Same reasoning as rev_growth_yoy. YoY net income growth is the most commonly
    cited earnings metric in analyst reports and earnings call commentary. It directly
    feeds consensus EPS estimates and is a primary driver of analyst price target
    revisions.

    Calculation: net_income.pct_change(4) per ticker. Clipped to [-5, 5].

18. eps_growth_yoy

    What it is:
    Year-over-year growth in diluted earnings per share: (EPS_t / EPS_{t-4}) - 1.
    Reflects both net income growth and share count changes (buybacks reduce share
    count, boosting EPS even if net income is flat).

    Why it is included:
    EPS is the primary metric tracked by equity analysts and the direct input to
    forward P/E valuation. EPS growth YoY captures the combined effect of profit
    growth and capital return programmes, making it a more shareholder-centric measure
    than net income growth alone. Companies that grow EPS faster than net income are
    effectively reducing their share count (a positive signal for remaining shareholders).

    Calculation: eps_diluted.pct_change(4) per ticker. Clipped to [-5, 5].

JOINING TO OTHER DATASETS
--------------------------
To merge fundamental features with the technical and sentiment datasets, join on
both Ticker and Date:

  merged = technical_dataset.merge(fundamental_dataset[['Ticker','Date'] + RATIO_COLS],
                                   on=['Ticker','Date'], how='left')
  merged = merged.merge(sentiment_dataset, on=['Ticker','Date'], how='left')

A left join on the technical dataset is recommended because technical_dataset.csv
defines the universe of valid prediction dates (after removing indicator warm-up rows
and trailing label rows). Residual NaN values after the join should be filled using
column-level medians computed on the training set only.
